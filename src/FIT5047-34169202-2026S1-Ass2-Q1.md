### Part (a): Validity and Satisfiability

**Lecture Recap:**

Before we begin, let us establish the logical definitions and rules we will use throughout the solution:
*   **Valid:** A formula is true in *all* possible models (truth assignments).
*   **Unsatisfiable:** A formula is false in *all* possible models.
*   **Satisfiable:** A formula is true in *at least one* model.
*   **Satisfiable but not valid:** A formula is true in some models, but false in others.
*   **Horn Clause:** A disjunction of literals (an OR-statement) that contains **at most one positive literal** (e.g., $\neg A \vee \neg B \vee C$).

</div>

**Strategy to prove validity and satisfiability** 
*   To prove a formula is **satisfiable**, we only need to find one **model** that makes it **$True$**. 
*   To prove a formula is **not valid**, we must find a counterexample: a model that makes it **$False$**. 
*   Since all three formulas are structured as **implications** ($Antecedent \Rightarrow Consequent$), the only way to make the entire formula **$False$** is to make the $Antecent = True$ and the $Consequent = False$.

#### 1. Formula $\phi_1$

$\phi_1 = ((A \vee (B \rightarrow C)) \wedge (\neg C \vee D)) \rightarrow ((A \rightarrow D) \vee (C \leftrightarrow E))$

**Let $\alpha$** $= ((A \vee (B \rightarrow C)) \wedge (\neg C \vee D))$ **the antecedent**
**Let $\beta$** $= ((A \rightarrow D) \vee (C \leftrightarrow E))$ **the consequent**
**So** $\phi_1 = \alpha \rightarrow \beta$

*   **Satisfiability Check:** 
    We can easily satisfy the implication by making the $\beta = True$.
    * Let $A = False$ and $D = True$. 
    * In the $\beta$ term $(A \rightarrow D)$ becomes $(False \rightarrow True) \equiv True$. 
    * Since the $\beta$ is $True$, the entire implication $\phi_1$ evaluates to $True$.
    * Thus, $\phi_1$ is **satisfiable**.

*   **Validity Check:** 
    We attempt to find a model where $\phi_1$ is $False$ ($\alpha = True$, $\beta = False$).
    *   For $\beta$ to be $False$, both parts of the OR must be $False$: 
        * $(A \rightarrow D)$ must be $False$ (meaning $A = True, D = False$) 
        * $(C \leftrightarrow E)$ must be $False$ (meaning $C \neq E$)
    *   For $\alpha$ to be $True$, both parts of the AND must be $True$. 
        * Given $D = False$, the second term $(\neg C \vee False)$ means $\neg C$ must be $True$, so $C = False$.
    *   Since $C \neq E$ from $\beta$ and $C = False$ from $\alpha$, then $E = True$.
    *   Looking at the first term of the $\alpha$: $(A \vee (B \rightarrow C))$. 
        * Since we know $A = True$, this term is automatically $True$. 
    *   **Counter-Model:** $\{A=True, B=True, C=False, D=False, E=True\}$.
        * In this model, $\alpha$ is $True$ and $\beta$ is $False$, yielding $True \rightarrow False \equiv False$.

#### 2. Formula $\phi_2$

$\phi_2 = (((\neg A \wedge B) \vee (C \rightarrow D)) \wedge (\neg E \vee A)) \rightarrow ((\neg D \vee F) \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B))$

**Let $\alpha$** $= (((\neg A \wedge B) \vee (C \rightarrow D)) \wedge (\neg E \vee A))$ **the antecedent**
**Let $\beta$** $= ((\neg D \vee F) \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B))$ **the consequent**
**So** $\phi_2 = \alpha \rightarrow \beta$

*   **Satisfiability Check:** 
    We can satisfy $\phi_2$ by making the $\alpha = False$.
    * Let $A = False$ and $E = True$. 
    * The term $(\neg E \vee A)$ becomes $(False \vee False) \equiv False$. 
    * Since the $\alpha$ is $False$, the main implication $\phi_2$ evaluates to $True$. 
    * Thus, $\phi_2$ is **satisfiable**.

*   **Validity Check:**
    We attempt to find a counterexample where $\alpha$ is $True$ and $\beta$ is $False$.
    *   Let $D = False$ and $F = False$. 
        * The $\beta$ becomes $(True \vee False) \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B) \equiv True \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B)$. 
        * For $\beta$ to be $False$, $(A \wedge B)$ must be $False$.
        * Let $A = False$, which satisfies $(A \wedge B) = False$. 
    *   Let $E = False$. 
        * The term in $\alpha$ $(\neg E \vee A)$ becomes $(True \vee False) \equiv True$.
    *   Now evaluate the remaining part of $\alpha$: $((\neg A \wedge B) \vee (C \rightarrow D))$. 
        * Since $A=False$, $\neg A = True$. 
        * Let $B = True$, so $(\neg A \wedge B) \equiv True$. 
        * Let $C = False$, so $(C \rightarrow False) \equiv True$. 
        * The $\alpha$ is $True$.
    *   **Counter-Model:** $\{A=False, B=True, C=False, D=False, E=False, F=False\}$.
        * In this model, $\alpha$ is $True$ and $\beta$ is $False$, yielding $True \rightarrow False \equiv False$.

#### 3. Formula $\phi_3$
$ \phi_3 = ((A \leftrightarrow B) \lor (C \rightarrow D)) \land ((\neg A \lor \neg D) \rightarrow (F \land \neg G)) $

**Let $\alpha$** $= ((A \leftrightarrow B) \lor (C \rightarrow D))$ **the left conjunct**
**Let $\beta$** $= ((\neg A \lor \neg D) \rightarrow (F \land \neg G))$ **the right conjunct**
**So** $\phi_3 = \alpha \wedge \beta$

*   **Satisfiability Check:**

    *   **The $\alpha$:** $(True \leftrightarrow True) \vee \dots \equiv True$. 
    *   **The $\beta$:** The antecedent is $(\neg True \vee \neg True) \equiv False$. An implication with a $False$ antecedent $(False \rightarrow \dots)$ is $True$.
    *   Since both $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are $True$, $\phi_3$ is $True$. 
    *   Thus, it is **satisfiable**.

*   **Validity Check:** 
    We can make $\phi_3$ $False$ simply by making the $\alpha = False$. 
    * Let $A=True, B=False$ (making $A \leftrightarrow B$ $False$). 
    * Let $C=True, D=False$ (making $C \rightarrow D$ $False$). 
    * The $\alpha$ is $False$, making the entire formula $\phi_3 = False$.

### Part (b): CNF Transformation and Horn Clauses

**Lecture Recap**

* **CNF Conversion:** Any logical sentence can be converted into **Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)**, a conjunction of disjunctions (AND of ORs) by applying four theoretical steps:
    1.  **Eliminate biconditionals $\leftrightarrow$:** $(A \leftrightarrow B) \equiv (A \to B) \land (B \to A)$
    2.  **Eliminate implications $\to$:** $(A \to B) \equiv (\neg A \lor B)$
    3.  **Move $\neg$ inwards:** Apply **De Morgan's laws** and **Double Negation**.
    4.  **Distribute $\lor$ over $\land$:** $A \lor (B \land C) \equiv (A \lor B) \land (A \lor C)$

* **Horn Clause:** A clause (disjunction of literals) containing **at most one positive literal**. These are critical for efficient inference in logical agents.

</div>

#### Transformation of $\phi_1$

$\phi_{1} = ((A \vee (B \rightarrow C)) \wedge (\neg C \vee D)) \rightarrow ((A \rightarrow D) \vee (C \leftrightarrow E))$

*   Original: $LHS \rightarrow RHS$. Eliminate implication: $\neg LHS \vee RHS$.
*   **Process LHS:** $LHS = ((A \vee \neg B \vee C) \wedge (\neg C \vee D))$. 
    Apply De Morgan's to find $\neg LHS$: 
    $\neg LHS = (\neg A \wedge B \wedge \neg C) \vee (C \wedge \neg D)$.
*   **Process RHS:** $RHS = (\neg A \vee D) \vee (C \leftrightarrow E)$. 
    Expand $\leftrightarrow$: $(\neg A \vee D) \vee ((\neg C \vee E) \wedge (\neg E \vee C))$.
    Distribute $\vee$: $RHS = (\neg A \vee D \vee \neg C \vee E) \wedge (\neg A \vee D \vee C \vee \neg E)$.
*   **Combine and Flatten:** We distribute $\neg LHS$ over the two $RHS$ conjunctions:
    *   *First conjunct:* $(\neg A \wedge B \wedge \neg C) \vee (C \wedge \neg D) \vee (\neg A \vee D \vee C \vee \neg E)$. 
        *Rule Highlight (Absorption):* Notice $(C \wedge \neg D) \vee D \equiv C \vee D$. The expression simplifies to $\neg A \vee C \vee D \vee \neg E$.
    *   *Second conjunct:* $(\neg A \wedge B \wedge \neg C) \vee (C \wedge \neg D) \vee (\neg A \vee D \vee \neg C \vee E)$.
        *Rule Highlight:* $(C \wedge \neg D) \vee \neg C \equiv \neg C \vee \neg D$. This leaves us with $D \vee \neg D$, which is a tautology ($\equiv True$), so this entire clause drops out.
*   **Final CNF for $\phi_1$:** $(\neg A \vee C \vee D \vee \neg E)$.
*   **Horn Clause Check:** It contains two positive literals ($C, D$), so it is **not** a Horn clause.

#### Transformation of $\phi_2$

$\phi_2 = (((\neg A \wedge B) \vee (C \rightarrow D)) \wedge (\neg E \vee A)) \rightarrow ((\neg D \vee F) \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B))$

Convert $\phi_{2} = LHS \rightarrow RHS \equiv \neg LHS \vee RHS$

*   We again apply $\neg LHS \vee RHS$.
*   **Process $\neg LHS$:** $\neg((( \neg A \wedge B) \vee (\neg C \vee D)) \wedge (\neg E \vee A))$.
    Pushing negations yields: $\neg LHS = (A \wedge C \wedge \neg D) \vee (\neg B \wedge C \wedge \neg D) \vee (E \wedge \neg A)$.
*   **Process RHS:** $(D \vee F) \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B)$.
    Converting this generates 5 distinct sub-clauses: $(D \vee A) \wedge (D \vee B) \wedge (\neg F \vee A) \wedge (\neg F \vee B) \wedge (\neg A \vee \neg B \vee \neg D \vee F)$.
*   **Combine and Flatten:** Distributing $\neg LHS$ over the $RHS$ yields 11 distinct clauses:
    1.  **$\neg A \vee \neg B \vee \neg D \vee F$ (Horn clause)**
    2.  $\neg B \vee A \vee D \vee E$
    3.  $A \vee C \vee D \vee E$
    4.  $B \vee C \vee D \vee E$
    5.  $\neg A \vee B \vee C \vee D$
    6.  $A \vee C \vee E \vee \neg F$
    7.  $A \vee \neg D \vee E \vee \neg F$
    8.  $B \vee C \vee E \vee \neg F$
    9.  $\neg A \vee B \vee C \vee \neg F$
    10. $B \vee \neg D \vee E \vee \neg F$
    11. **$\neg A \vee B \vee \neg D \vee \neg F$ (Horn clause)**
*   **Horn Clause Check:** Clauses 1 and 11 contain exactly one positive literal each ($F$ and $B$, respectively), making them Horn clauses.
    * **Clause 1:** $(\neg A \vee \neg B \vee \neg D \vee F)$ (One positive literal: $F$)
    * **Clause 11:** $(\neg A \vee B \vee \neg D \vee \neg F)$ (One positive literal: $B$)

#### Transformation of $\phi_3$
$\phi_{3} = ((A \leftrightarrow B) \vee (C \rightarrow D)) \wedge ((\neg A \vee \neg D) \rightarrow (F \wedge \neg G))$
*   Because $\phi_3$ is an AND statement between two large expressions, we process them as Conjunct 1 and Conjunct 2 separately.
*   **Conjunct 1:** $(A \leftrightarrow B) \vee (C \rightarrow D)$. 
    Eliminate equivalents: $((A \vee \neg B) \wedge (\neg A \vee B)) \vee (\neg C \vee D)$.
    Distribute OR over AND: $(A \vee \neg B \vee \neg C \vee D) \wedge (\neg A \vee B \vee \neg C \vee D)$.
*   **Conjunct 2:** $(\neg A \vee \neg D) \rightarrow (F \wedge \neg G)$.
    Eliminate implication: $\neg(\neg A \vee \neg D) \vee (F \wedge \neg G)$.
    Apply De Morgan's: $(A \wedge D) \vee (F \wedge \neg G)$.
    Distribute OR over AND: $(A \vee F) \wedge (A \vee \neg G) \wedge (D \vee F) \wedge (D \vee \neg G)$.
*   **Final CNF for $\phi_3$ (combining all 6 clauses):**
    1.  $A \vee \neg B \vee \neg C \vee D$
    2.  $\neg A \vee B \vee \neg C \vee D$
    3.  $A \vee F$
    4.  **$A \vee \neg G$ (Horn clause)**
    5.  $D \vee F$
    6.  **$D \vee \neg G$ (Horn clause)**
*   **Horn Clause Check:** Clauses 4 and 6 contain exactly one positive literal ($A$ and $D$), so they are Horn clauses.
*   **Horn Clauses in $\phi_3$:**
    *   $(A \vee \neg G)$ (One positive literal: $A$)
    *   $(D \vee \neg G)$ (One positive literal: $D$)

### Part (c): Evaluating $\phi_2$ when $F$ is always False

**Lecture Recap:**

* **Vacuous Truth (Implications):** An implication ($P \rightarrow Q$) is evaluated as True unless the premise ($P$) is True and the conclusion ($Q$) is False. If the premise is False, the implication is **vacuously True**.
* **Logical Inference (Entailment):** We say $\alpha \models \beta$ if $\beta$ is True in every **interpretation** where $\alpha$ is True. If satisfying models exist with conflicting truth values for a variable, no definitive entailment exists.

</div>

Given $\phi_{2} = (((\neg A \wedge B) \vee (C \rightarrow D)) \wedge (\neg E \vee A)) \rightarrow ((\neg D \vee F) \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B))$. 

Assume $F = False$.

#### (i) What is the effect on satisfiability?
*   Substitute $F = False$ into the original implication $\phi_2$.
*   The RHS was $((\neg D \vee F) \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B))$. 
*   With $F = False$, this becomes $((\neg D \vee False) \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B))$, which simplifies directly to $(\neg D \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B))$.
*   The modified formula $\phi_2'$ is: $LHS \rightarrow (\neg D \leftrightarrow (A \wedge B))$.

            border-radius: 8px;">
    
🎯 **Conclusion:** The formula **remains satisfiable**. We can still trivially satisfy it by forcing the LHS to be False (for example, setting $A = False$ and $E = True$), which makes the entire implication True regardless of the RHS.

</div>

#### (ii) Can you infer anything about A or B? Justify

**Lecture Recap:** 
* In propositional logic, to "infer" or unconditionally entail a proposition like $A$, it must be True in *all* satisfying models of the formula (i.e., $\phi_2' \models A$).

</div>

*   Let us test different satisfying truth assignments (models) for our modified $\phi_2'$:
    *   **Model 1 ($A$ is False, $B$ is True):** Let $A = False, E = True$. The LHS evaluates to False. The implication holds True. Thus, $A$ can be False.
    *   **Model 2 ($A$ is True, $B$ is True):** Let $A=True, B=True, C=False, D=False, E=True$. LHS becomes $True$. RHS becomes $\neg False \leftrightarrow (True \wedge True) \equiv True \leftrightarrow True \equiv True$. $True \rightarrow True$ holds True.
    *   **Model 3 ($A$ is True, $B$ is False):** Let $A=True, B=False, C=False, D=True, E=True$. LHS becomes $True$. RHS becomes $\neg True \leftrightarrow (True \wedge False) \equiv False \leftrightarrow False \equiv True$. $True \rightarrow True$ holds True.

**🎯 Conclusion:** **No, we cannot unconditionally infer anything about the truth values of $A$ or $B$**. Because there exist perfectly valid, satisfying models where $A$ and $B$ can independently take either True or False values, the knowledge base is not strict enough to force a specific truth assignment for them.
