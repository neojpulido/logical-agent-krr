**Lecture Recap:** 
*   **Forward Chaining** is a **data-driven** algorithm. It starts with the known facts and applies **Modus Ponens** iteratively to trigger rules and generate new facts until the goal is reached or no more facts can be inferred. 
*   **Definite Clauses:** Forward chaining expects the knowledge base to consist of Definite Clauses (rules with exactly one positive literal in the conclusion). Rules $R9$ and $R10$ contain disjunctions ($OR$ / $\vee$) in their conclusions, meaning they are not Horn clauses and cannot be processed by standard forward chaining. We will evaluate the counts for the valid definite clauses ($R1$ through $R7$).
*   **Algorithm Mechanics:** We maintain a `Count` of how many premises are unmet for each rule. When a fact is popped from the `Queue`, we decrement the count for any rule requiring that fact. If a rule's count reaches $0$, its conclusion is added to the `Queue`.

</div>

*   **Initial state:** 
    We initialize the `Count` for the premises of our definite clauses: 
    * $R1$ has 3 premises ($P, T, NS$). 
    * $R2, R3, R4, R5, R6, R7$ each has 1 premise. 
*   **Queue:** 
    Populated with the initial facts: $[F, E]$.

### **Q2.3: Backward Chaining**

<div style="background-color: #f8f9fa; 
            border-left: 6px solid #004a99; 
            padding: 20px; 
            margin: 20px 0; 
            border-radius: 4px; 
            font-family: sans-serif;">

**Lecture Recap:**

*   **Backward Chaining** is a **goal-driven** algorithm. It works backward from the query $q$ (the Goal). 
*   **Mechanism:** To prove a goal, the algorithm checks if it is already a known fact. If not, it finds all rules that have the goal as their conclusion (the right-hand side of the $\Rightarrow$) and recursively attempts to prove every premise of those rules (the left-hand side) as new subgoals.
*   **Efficiency:** It avoids loops by checking if a subgoal is already on the stack, and it avoids repeated work by checking if a subgoal has already been proven true in a previous branch.

</div>

**Execution Steps:**
1.  **Current Goal:** **$S$**
    *   Is $S$ a known fact? No.
    *   Find a rule concluding $S$: **R1** ($P \wedge T \wedge NS \Rightarrow S$).
    *   To prove $S$, we must prove the subgoals: **$P, T, NS$**.

2.  **Evaluate Subgoal 1:** **$P$**
    *   Is $P$ a known fact? No.
    *   Find a rule concluding $P$: **R5** ($NS \Rightarrow P$).
    *   New Subgoal to prove $P$: **$NS$**.
    *   **Evaluate Subgoal 1.1:** **$NS$**
        *   Is $NS$ a known fact? No.
        *   Find a rule concluding $NS$: **R4** ($E \Rightarrow NS$).
        *   New Subgoal to prove $NS$: **$E$**.
        *   **Evaluate Subgoal 1.1.1:** **$E$**
            *   Is $E$ a known fact? **Yes!**
        *   *Result:* $E$ is true $\rightarrow$ $NS$ is true $\rightarrow$ **$P$ is true**. 

3.  **Evaluate Subgoal 2:** **$T$** (Returning to R1's premises)
    *   Is $T$ a known fact? No.
    *   Find a rule concluding $T$: **R3** ($H \Rightarrow T$).
    *   New Subgoal to prove $T$: **$H$**.
    *   **Evaluate Subgoal 2.1:** **$H$**
        *   Is $H$ a known fact? No.
        *   Find a rule concluding $H$: **R2** ($F \Rightarrow H$).
        *   New Subgoal to prove $H$: **$F$**.
        *   **Evaluate Subgoal 2.1.1:** **$F$**
            *   Is $F$ a known fact? **Yes!**
        *   *Result:* $F$ is true $\rightarrow$ $H$ is true $\rightarrow$ **$T$ is true**.

4.  **Evaluate Subgoal 3:** **$NS$** (Returning to R1's premises)
    *   Is $NS$ a known fact? No.
    *   *Theory Check:* Has this subgoal already been proven? **Yes.** We proved $NS$ in Step 2 while proving $P$.
    *   *Result:* The algorithm immediately returns true without recalculating. **$NS$ is true**.

### Q2.4: Discussion

#### (i) Does forward chaining stop early in this scenario? Why?
**Yes, forward chaining stops early.**
*   **Justification:** 
    The forward chaining algorithm includes an explicit termination condition: 
    * `if p == q then return true` 
      * $p$ is the fact currently popped from the queue
      * $q$ is the goal. 
    * In Step 6 of our trace, processing $P$ inferred both $S$ and $W$. Both were added to the queue. 
    * In Step 7, $S$ was popped from the queue. Because $S$ matched our goal, the algorithm successfully stopped immediately. 
    * It *stopped early* because it did not waste computational resources evaluating the remaining items in the queue (like popping $W$) or attempting to trigger further rules down the line. 
    * It also naturally ignored irrelevant paths (like $R7$) because they were never triggered by the data.
  
#### (ii) Could adding $\neg M$ or $A$ improve inference? Why or why not?
**No, adding $\neg M$ or $A$ would not improve inference.**
*   **Justification:** 
    *  **Relevance to Goal:** The goal $S$ is already perfectly provable using only the minimal initial facts ($F, E$). Adding more facts to the KB cannot make the proof of $S$ any more successful; it would only expand the state space and the queue size, mathematically *decreasing* algorithmic efficiency.
    *  **Structural Limitations:** Standard Forward and Backward Chaining algorithms are designed to operate exclusively on Horn Knowledge Bases (Definite Clauses containing positive literals). Adding a negative literal ($\neg M$) cannot be utilized by basic chaining rules, and rule $R7$ requires a positive $M$ to trigger.
    *  **Disconnected Rules:** The symbol $A$ relies on rule $R8$. As established in Q2.1, R8 is discarded because its premise ("**sneeze in spring**") is unrepresented. Consequently, $A$ is entirely disconnected from the logic network mapping to $S$. Injecting it provides zero heuristic or logical value.
